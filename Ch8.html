Chapter 8: Identity, Culture & Challenges to the West

The Politics of Who We Are

The Rise of Identity Politics

Identity politics is a style of politics that seeks to advance the interests of a particular group in society, based on a shared sense of collective identity (e.g., based on gender, religion, ethnicity, or race). It challenges the idea that politics should only be about universal values like liberty and justice.

It is a shift from the politics of "what we want" (interests) to the politics of "who we are" (identity).

The Resurgence of Religion

For much of the 20th century, it was assumed that modernization would lead to secularization (the decline of religion). Instead, the late 20th century saw a powerful resurgence of religion in politics, often in the form of religious fundamentalism. Click to explore its key features.

Religion as Politics

+

Fundamentalism is not just about personal faith; it is an ideology that seeks to organize all aspects of society—political, social, and economic—according to religious principles.

Anti-Modernism

+

Fundamentalists often reject the core values of the modern, secular West, such as liberalism, individualism, and materialism. They see these values as corrupting and morally bankrupt.

Militancy

+

While not all fundamentalists are violent, they share a militant conviction in the absolute truth of their sacred texts and a desire to see this truth triumph over competing worldviews.

The 'Clash of Civilizations'?

Samuel Huntington's controversial 1993 thesis argued that the key source of conflict in the post-Cold War world would be cultural, not ideological or economic. He predicted that conflict would occur along the "fault lines" between the world's major civilizations.

Huntington's thesis remains highly influential but is also heavily criticized for oversimplifying complex identities and for potentially encouraging a self-fulfilling prophecy of conflict.

Challenges to the West

The era of unchallenged Western dominance is being contested from multiple directions, both from within and from without.

Postcolonialism

This body of thought seeks to expose and challenge the enduring cultural and psychological legacies of colonialism. It highlights how Western cultural norms are often treated as universal, while non-Western cultures are marginalized.

Asian Values

A set of values promoted by some Asian leaders that supposedly reflect the unique culture of East Asia. These values—such as social harmony, respect for authority, and family loyalty—are often presented as an alternative to Western liberal individualism.

Multiculturalism

A challenge from within. Multiculturalism celebrates cultural diversity and argues that minority cultures should be protected and recognized. This challenges the traditional liberal idea of a single, universal citizenship where cultural differences are treated as private matters.
