Chapter 14: International Law

Order and Justice in a Global Age

What is International Law?

International law is the set of rules, norms, and standards generally accepted as binding between states. It is a key institution of world order, providing a framework for cooperation and a basis for resolving disputes. But where does this law come from?

Treaties

Formal, written agreements between states that are legally binding. The most important source of international law.

Customary Law

Law that derives from the past practices of states, which they follow out of a sense of legal obligation.

General Principles

Underlying principles of law that are recognized by most legal systems around the world.

Judicial Decisions

Rulings from international courts, which help to interpret and clarify the law.

The Great Debate: Is it Really Law?

The biggest controversy surrounding international law is whether it can be considered "law" in the same way as domestic law. This debate pits realists against liberals and other theorists.

The Sceptical View (Realists)

Realists argue that international law is not really law because there is no world government to enforce it. In an anarchic world, states will only obey international law when it is in their national interest to do so. For realists, international law is a tool of the powerful.

The Proponent View (Liberals & Others)

Liberals argue that international law is a crucial feature of global politics. They point out that states obey international law most of the time because it is in their long-term interest to live in a stable, orderly world. Law provides a basis for cooperation and helps to build international norms.

Adjudicating International Law

The growth of international law has been accompanied by the creation of international courts to interpret and apply it. Two of the most important are the ICJ and the ICC.

The International Court of Justice (ICJ)

+

The principal judicial organ of the United Nations, also known as the "World Court." Its role is to settle legal disputes submitted to it by states. However, its jurisdiction is voluntary; it can only hear cases if both states involved agree to participate.

The International Criminal Court (ICC)

+

Established in 2002, the ICC is the world's first permanent court for prosecuting individuals accused of the most serious international crimes: genocide, war crimes, and crimes against humanity. It is a court of last resort, only acting when national courts are unable or unwilling to do so.

The Laws of War

One of the oldest and most important branches of international law is the attempt to regulate the conduct of armed conflict. This body of law, also known as International Humanitarian Law, seeks to bring a measure of humanity to the inhumanity of war.

The Geneva Conventions

A series of treaties, most famously updated in 1949 after WWII, that establish the standards for the humane treatment of the victims of war, including prisoners of war (POWs) and civilians.

War Crimes

A serious violation of the laws of war, giving rise to individual criminal responsibility. The concept of war crimes challenges the idea that soldiers are just "following orders" and holds individuals accountable for atrocities committed during conflict.
