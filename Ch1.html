Chapter 1: Introducing Global Politics

A New and Interconnected World

What is Global Politics?

Global politics is a field of study that examines political issues and relationships that stretch across national borders and have implications for the whole world. It signifies a fundamental shift from the traditional focus on "international" politics.

International Politics

The traditional view. Politics is a "billiard ball" world of states interacting with each other, but remaining separate, sovereign entities.

The Great Globalization Debate

Is globalization a revolutionary new era or just business as usual? Scholars are deeply divided. Here are the three main perspectives.

The Hyperglobalists

Believe we now live in a "borderless world" where the nation-state is becoming obsolete. For them, globalization is a new epoch in human history, driven by the logic of the global market.

The Sceptics

Argue that globalization is a myth. They believe the world is no more interconnected than it was in the 19th century and is now breaking up into regional economic blocs (e.g., Europe, North America, East Asia).

The Transformationalists

Take the middle ground. They see globalization as a real and profound force that is reshaping politics and society, but in complex and uncertain ways. It's not the end of the state, but a transformation of its power and role.

A New World Order?

Globalization has sparked intense debates about the future of global politics. Two influential, and opposing, theories emerged in the post-Cold War era.

The "End of History"

Francis Fukuyama's theory that the end of the Cold War signaled the final victory of Western liberal democracy as the ultimate form of human government. History, as a great clash of ideologies, was over.

The "Clash of Civilizations"

Samuel Huntington's counter-argument. He predicted that the fundamental source of future conflict would not be ideological or economic, but cultural, with clashes occurring between major world civilizations (e.g., Western, Islamic, Confucian).
