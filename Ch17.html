Chapter 17: Gender in Global Politics

A Gendered World

Sex vs. Gender

Feminist analysis begins with a crucial distinction between 'sex' and 'gender'. Understanding this difference is key to seeing how global politics is 'gendered'.

Sex

A biological category. It refers to the physical and genetic differences between males and females.

Gender

A social and cultural construct. It refers to the different roles, expectations, and identities that society attaches to being male or female. Gender is not what we are; it is what we do.

Feminist Theories of Global Politics

There is no single feminist theory. Different feminist traditions offer different explanations for gender inequality and different visions for how to achieve a more just world.

Liberal Feminism: Equal Rights

This is the mainstream approach. It argues that gender inequality is caused by legal and institutional discrimination against women. The solution is to remove these barriers and ensure that women have the same rights and opportunities as men.

Critical Feminism: Deconstructing Patriarchy

This perspective argues that gender inequality is rooted in a deeper structure of male dominance called 'patriarchy'. It is not just about equal rights; it is about challenging and overthrowing the social, economic, and political structures that perpetuate gender oppression.

Postcolonial Feminism: Intersecting Oppressions

This perspective criticizes mainstream feminism for often focusing on the experiences of white, middle-class women in the West. It highlights how gender intersects with other forms of oppression, such as racism and colonialism, and argues that the experience of being a woman is very different in the Global South.

Gender in Action: Economy and War

Applying a 'gender lens' to global politics reveals hidden inequalities and provides new insights into key issues.

Women in the Global Economy

Globalization has been a double-edged sword for women. While it has created new employment opportunities, women are often concentrated in low-paid, insecure jobs in industries like textiles and electronics. This is sometimes called the 'feminization of poverty'.

Gender and War

War affects men and women in profoundly different ways. While men are more likely to be combatants, women and children make up the vast majority of civilian casualties and refugees. Furthermore, sexual violence, especially rape, has increasingly been used as a systematic weapon of war.

Institutionalizing Gender Equity

The international community has taken significant steps to recognize and address gender inequality, a process often called 'gender mainstreaming'.

The UN and Women's Rights

The United Nations has been at the forefront of this effort. The 1979 Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women (CEDAW) is often described as an international bill of rights for women. More recently, UN Women was created to coordinate the UN's work on gender equality.

UN Security Council Resolution 1325

This landmark 2000 resolution recognized the disproportionate impact of war on women and called for women to be given a greater role in peace negotiations and post-conflict reconstruction. It marked a major step in linking women's experiences to matters of international peace and security.
