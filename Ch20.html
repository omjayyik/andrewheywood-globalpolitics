Building Blocs in a Globalizing World

What is Regionalism?

Regionalism is a process through which states in a particular geographic area deepen their cooperation and integration. It can range from simple economic cooperation to the creation of powerful, state-like supranational bodies. Is regionalism a stepping stone to globalization, or a stumbling block that creates a more divided world?

Federalism: A Political Project

This view sees regionalism as a political project driven by a desire to create a larger political union (a "United States of Europe," for example). It is a top-down process led by political elites.

Functionalism: Form Follows Function

This view sees regional integration as a practical response to common problems. Cooperation starts in specific, technical, "functional" areas (like managing coal and steel production). This builds trust and creates a need for further cooperation.

Neofunctionalism: The 'Spillover' Effect

This theory builds on functionalism. It argues that cooperation in one area will create pressures for cooperation in other areas, a process called 'spillover'. For example, a single market creates a need for a single currency, which in turn creates a need for a common fiscal policy.

Case Study: The European Union

The European Union (EU) is the most advanced and institutionalized example of regional integration in the world. It has evolved from a simple trade agreement into a powerful political and economic union that possesses many of the features of a state.

Key Institutions

European Commission: The executive body, proposes legislation.
Council of the European Union: Represents member state governments.
European Parliament: The directly elected legislative body.
European Court of Justice: The supreme court for EU law.

Regionalism Beyond Europe

While the EU is unique, regionalism has become a global phenomenon. A "new regionalism" has emerged since the 1990s, driven by globalization and the end of the Cold War.

NAFTA / USMCA

The North American Free Trade Agreement (now the United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement) is a free trade zone, primarily focused on economic integration.

ASEAN

The Association of Southeast Asian Nations focuses on economic, political, and security cooperation, but strongly emphasizes the principle of non-interference in the internal affairs of its members (the "ASEAN way").

African Union (AU)

The successor to the Organization of African Unity, the AU has an ambitious agenda that includes promoting democracy, human rights, and development. It has asserted the right to intervene in member states in cases of genocide and war crimes.
