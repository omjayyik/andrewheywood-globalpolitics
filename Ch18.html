Chapter 18: International Organizations & the UN

Governing a Globalized World

The Rise of International Organizations

International organizations (IOs) are bodies set up by states to work together on common problems. They are a central feature of global governance, but their power and influence are highly contested. Are they independent actors, or merely tools of powerful states?

The Realist View

Realists see IOs as instruments of state power. They have no independent influence and are only effective when they serve the interests of the most powerful states.

The Liberal View

Liberals see IOs as crucial arenas for cooperation. They help states to overcome mistrust, build norms of behavior, and achieve shared goals that they could not achieve alone.

The United Nations

Founded in 1945, the United Nations is the world's only truly global organization. Its primary mission is to maintain international peace and security, but its work encompasses a vast range of global issues. Click to explore its principal organs.

The Security Council

+

The UN's most powerful body, responsible for maintaining international peace and security. It has 15 members, but its power is concentrated in the hands of the five permanent, veto-wielding members (P5): the USA, Russia, China, the UK, and France.

The General Assembly

+

The UN's "parliament of nations." It comprises all 193 member states, each with one vote. Its resolutions are not legally binding, but they carry significant moral weight.

The Secretariat & Secretary-General

+

The UN's administrative arm, headed by the Secretary-General, who acts as the organization's chief diplomat and public face.

The Economic and Social Council (ECOSOC)

+

Coordinates the economic and social work of the UN and its vast network of specialized agencies, such as the World Health Organization (WHO) and UNICEF.

UN Peacekeeping

One of the UN's most visible and controversial roles is peacekeeping. UN "blue helmets" are deployed to conflict zones to help control conflict and stabilize post-conflict situations. The nature of peacekeeping has evolved dramatically.

Traditional Peacekeeping

Developed during the Cold War. Peacekeepers were lightly armed and were only deployed with the consent of the warring parties to monitor a ceasefire.

Post-Cold War Peacekeeping

Missions became larger, more complex, and more dangerous. "Peace enforcement" operations often involve deploying to ongoing civil wars without the full consent of all parties and using force to protect civilians and disarm combatants.

The Future of the UN: Reform or Decline?

The UN faces a crisis of legitimacy. Many argue that it is an outdated institution, designed for the world of 1945, not the 21st century. This has led to widespread calls for reform.

The Case for the UN

Supporters argue that for all its flaws, the UN remains an indispensable body. It provides a vital forum for diplomacy, coordinates global efforts to tackle problems like poverty and disease, and is a symbol of a shared global community.

The Need for Reform

The most urgent debate is about reforming the Security Council. Critics argue that its composition, especially the P5 and their veto power, is anachronistic and undemocratic. However, any attempt to change the veto is certain to be vetoed by the P5 themselves.
