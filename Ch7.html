Chapter 7: The Nation in a Global Age

An Enduring Idea

Nation vs. State

The terms 'nation' and 'state' are often used interchangeably, but they refer to very different concepts. Understanding this distinction is fundamental to the study of global politics.

The Nation: A Cultural Community

The nation is a cultural and psychological entity. It is a group of people who share a common language, religion, history, or culture, and who see themselves as a distinct political community. It is a "psycho-political" construct.

The State: A Political Entity

The state is a political and legal entity. It is a sovereign body with a defined territory, a permanent population, and an effective government. It is the primary actor in international law.

The Cultural Nation: A Community of Descent

This view sees nations as organic communities that are bound together by a shared ethnic or cultural heritage. National identity is inherited, not chosen. This can lead to an exclusive and ethnically-defined nationalism.

The Political Nation: A Community of Citizens

This view sees nations as social constructs, bound together by a shared sense of citizenship and a commitment to common political values and principles (patriotism). National identity is inclusive and can be adopted by anyone who chooses to embrace these values.

The Fate of the Nation

Has globalization made the nation obsolete? Or has it provoked a powerful nationalist backlash?

Thesis: The Rise of Cosmopolitanism

This argument suggests that globalization, migration, and cultural exchange are eroding traditional national identities and fostering a "post-national" sense of identity, where people see themselves as "citizens of the world."

Antithesis: The Nationalist Backlash

This argument suggests that for many, globalization is a profound threat to their culture and way of life. This has fueled a powerful resurgence of nationalism, often in the form of populism and anti-immigrant sentiment, as people seek the security of their national community.
