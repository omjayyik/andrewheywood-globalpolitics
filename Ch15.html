A World of Haves and Have-Nots

Understanding Poverty and Justice

The existence of extreme poverty in a world of unprecedented wealth raises profound questions about global justice. But how do we define poverty, and what does justice mean in a global context?

Absolute Poverty

A standard of poverty that is based on the minimum income needed to meet basic human needs for food, shelter, and clothing. The World Bank defines this as living on less than $1.90 a day.

Relative Poverty

A standard of poverty in which people are deprived of the living standards and amenities that are customary in their society. It is a measure of inequality, not just destitution.

The Liberal View: Internal Factors

This perspective argues that poverty is caused by internal factors within developing countries themselves. Modernization theory, for example, suggests that poverty is caused by cultural barriers to modernization, such as traditional beliefs and corrupt institutions. The solution is for developing countries to embrace free-market capitalism.

The Structuralist View: External Factors

This perspective, rooted in Marxism, argues that poverty is caused by the exploitative structure of the global capitalist system. Dependency theory suggests that the wealthy "core" countries have underdeveloped the "periphery" countries, locking them into a cycle of poverty and dependence.

The Great Aid Debate

Does foreign aid help or hinder development? This is one of the most contentious issues in global politics.

The Case FOR Aid

Supporters argue that aid is a moral imperative and a practical necessity. It can save lives, fund crucial infrastructure projects, and help countries escape the "poverty trap." The UN has long called for rich countries to commit 0.7% of their national income to development assistance.

The Case AGAINST Aid

Critics argue that aid is often ineffective and can even be harmful. They claim that it fosters dependency, props up corrupt governments, and distorts local markets. Some argue that free trade, not aid, is the real key to development.

From State to Market

The dominant approach to development has shifted dramatically over the past several decades, from a belief in state-led industrialization to a faith in the power of the free market.

Orthodox Development (pre-1980s)

The early model of development focused on state-led industrialization, often through protectionist policies designed to shield "infant industries" from foreign competition.

Neoliberal Development (post-1980s)

The rise of the "Washington Consensus" marked a shift to a market-led model. This approach emphasizes free trade, privatization, and deregulation as the universal recipe for economic growth, often imposed on developing countries through IMF and World Bank structural adjustment programs.
