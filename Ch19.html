Chapter 19: Global Governance & the Bretton Woods System

Managing a Complex World Economy

What is Global Governance?

Global governance refers to the various ways in which the world is managed, despite the absence of a world government. It is a complex, overlapping web of formal institutions, informal mechanisms, and relationships between states, international organizations, and non-state actors that work to tackle collective problems.

Global Government

A hierarchical system with a single, authoritative world state. This does NOT exist.

Global Governance

A horizontal system of cooperation and negotiation among multiple actors to create order and solve problems. This is the reality of our world.

The Bretton Woods System

The modern system of global economic governance was born in 1944 at Bretton Woods. Its goal was to create a stable world economy that balanced free trade with national autonomy, a system known as 'embedded liberalism'.

International Monetary Fund (IMF)

Designed to promote international monetary cooperation and exchange rate stability. It provides short-term loans to countries with balance-of-payments problems.

World Bank

Initially created to fund post-war reconstruction, its role shifted to providing long-term loans for development projects in the Global South.

GATT / WTO

The General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (and its successor, the World Trade Organization) was established to create a forum for negotiating the reduction of trade barriers.

From Embedded Liberalism to Neoliberalism

The Bretton Woods system broke down in the 1970s. Since then, global economic governance has been dominated by a more market-fundamentalist approach known as neoliberalism, most famously expressed in the 'Washington Consensus'.

The Washington Consensus: A Recipe for Development?

This was a set of free-market policies that the IMF and World Bank promoted as the solution for developing countries. Key elements included:

  • Privatization: Selling off state-owned industries.
  • Deregulation: Removing government controls on the economy.
  • Free Trade: Opening up markets to international competition.
  • Fiscal Austerity: Cutting public spending to reduce deficits.

Reforming Global Governance

The institutions of global economic governance face a deep crisis of legitimacy. They have been criticized for being undemocratic, unaccountable, and for promoting policies that benefit the rich at the expense of the poor.

The Democratic Deficit

Key decisions are made by powerful, unelected bureaucrats. Furthermore, voting power in the IMF and World Bank is based on financial contributions, giving a huge advantage to the USA and other wealthy countries.

The Rise of the BRICS

Emerging economies like Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa are demanding a greater voice in global economic governance. Frustrated with the slow pace of reform, they have created alternative institutions like the New Development Bank.
