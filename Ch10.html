Chapter 10: War and Peace

The Ultimate Questions of Global Politics

The Changing Face of War

War, as Clausewitz famously said, is "the continuation of politics by other means." But the nature of war has changed dramatically over time. The era of conventional, state-versus-state warfare has increasingly been replaced by new, more complex forms of conflict.

Conventional Warfare

Fought by uniformed soldiers from recognizable states, who target each other's military forces. There is a clear distinction between soldiers and civilians. This was the dominant form of war for centuries.

The 'New' Wars

Characterized by the rise of non-state actors (guerrillas, terrorists), the blurring of lines between soldiers and civilians, and the use of identity politics to mobilize populations. These are often civil wars, not wars between states.

The Realist View: An Inevitable Tragedy

For realists, war is an unavoidable feature of an anarchic world system. States are driven by self-interest and a lust for power, and in a self-help system with no world government, war is the ultimate tool for ensuring survival. The "security dilemma" ensures that even defensive military build-ups can be seen as a threat, leading to arms races and conflict.

The Liberal View: A Solvable Problem

Liberals are more optimistic. They argue that while war is always possible, it can be prevented. Their "democratic peace thesis" holds that liberal democracies do not go to war with one another. They also believe that economic interdependence and international organizations make war less likely by making cooperation more beneficial.

Critical Views: A Product of Systems

Critical theorists see war as a product of deeper structures of power. Marxists argue that war is a product of the global capitalist system, driven by competition for resources and markets. Postcolonial thinkers highlight how military interventions are often a new form of imperialism.

The Ethics of War: Just War Theory

Is it ever morally acceptable to go to war? Just war theory provides a framework for judging the morality of conflict. It sets out ethical conditions for both going to war and for conduct within war.

Jus ad Bellum (Justice of War)

The conditions for justly going to war. This includes having a just cause (like self-defense), being a last resort, having a reasonable prospect of success, and being declared by a legitimate authority.

Jus in Bello (Justice in War)

The conditions for just conduct during a war. This includes the principles of discrimination (not targeting non-combatants) and proportionality (using no more force than is necessary to achieve military goals).

Pathways to Peace

How can peace and security be achieved in a turbulent world? Different approaches offer different strategies.

Balance of Power

The classic realist solution. Peace is maintained when power is distributed in such a way that no single state is strong enough to dominate all others. This creates a deterrent effect.

Collective Security

The liberal ideal, embodied in the United Nations. The idea is that an attack on any one state is an attack on all states, which will act together to repel the aggressor. "All for one, and one for all."

Critical Security Studies

This approach challenges the traditional, state-centric view of security. It argues for "human security," focusing on protecting individuals from threats like poverty, disease, and environmental degradation, not just military threats.
