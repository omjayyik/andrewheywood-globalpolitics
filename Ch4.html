Economic Liberalism (Capitalism)

The dominant theory. It argues that markets are the most efficient mechanism for allocating resources. The pursuit of private profit will ultimately lead to wealth for all.

  • Key Idea: The "invisible hand" of the market creates prosperity.
  • Role of the State: Minimal. The state should not interfere with the natural logic of the market.
  • Global View: Believes in free trade and open markets, creating a world of positive-sum gains where everyone can benefit.

Mercantilism (Neomercantilism)

This theory links economic power to state power. It argues that the main purpose of the economy is to serve the national interest and build a strong state.

  • Key Idea: Economic strength is critical for national security.
  • Role of the State: Central. The state must direct the economy to protect national industries.
  • Global View: Sees the global economy as an arena of competition. Believes in protectionism (e.g., tariffs) to shield domestic industries from foreign rivals.

Marxism (Neo-Marxism)

Views the global economy as an inherently unequal and exploitative system, structured to benefit the capitalist class.

  • Key Idea: Capitalism is a system of class oppression.
  • Role of the State: The state is not a neutral actor; it serves the interests of the dominant capitalist class.
  • Global View: Sees a world divided into a wealthy "core" and an exploited "periphery." Global institutions like the IMF and World Bank are seen as tools of this exploitation.

Building the Post-War Order

To prevent a repeat of the economic chaos that led to WWII, allied powers met at Bretton Woods in 1944. They created a new system of "embedded liberalism" that balanced free markets with state intervention to ensure stability.

The IMF

The International Monetary Fund was created to ensure stable exchange rates (pegged to the US dollar) and provide short-term loans to countries facing currency crises.

The World Bank

The International Bank for Reconstruction and Development was created to fund post-war reconstruction in Europe, and later shifted its mission to promoting development in the Global South.

GATT (later WTO)

The General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade was established to reduce trade barriers and promote free trade. It was later replaced by the more powerful World Trade Organization in 1995.

The Neoliberal Turn

The Bretton Woods system collapsed in the 1970s. It was replaced by a more radical, market-fundamentalist version of capitalism known as neoliberalism, which has dominated global economic policy ever since.

Deregulation

Removing state controls on markets, especially financial markets, to allow capital to flow freely across borders.

Privatization

Transferring state-owned assets and services (like utilities or airlines) to the private sector.

The "Washington Consensus"

A set of free-market policies promoted by the IMF and World Bank as the required conditions for developing countries to receive loans, often involving deep cuts to public spending (austerity).

Crises of Globalization

Neoliberal globalization has been marked by a series of profound crises that have challenged its core principles and exposed its vulnerabilities.

The 2008 Financial Crisis

Beginning in the US subprime mortgage market, this crisis spread globally, triggering the worst recession since the 1930s. It exposed the dangers of unregulated financial markets and led to massive state bailouts of banks, challenging the idea that markets are self-regulating.

The Rise of Economic Nationalism

Growing inequality and job losses in developed countries have fueled a backlash against globalization. This is seen in movements like Brexit and the "America First" policies of Donald Trump, which challenge free trade and favor protectionism.
