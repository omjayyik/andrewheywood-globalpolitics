Chapter 6: Society in a Global Age

Beyond the State

The Rise of Global Civil Society

Global civil society refers to a realm of private, non-state groups and associations that operate on a global scale. These actors—from community groups to international NGOs—have created a new sphere of politics that exists alongside the formal world of states and international organizations.

It is the "third pillar" of global governance, alongside the state and the market.

Who are the Global Actors?

Non-governmental organizations (NGOs) are the heart of global civil society. They are private, non-profit bodies that are independent of government control and dedicated to advocating for a particular cause. Click to explore their roles.

Advocacy & Agenda-Setting

+

NGOs act as "norm entrepreneurs," using media campaigns and lobbying to bring issues to the attention of the global public and policymakers. Example: Greenpeace's campaigns against whaling.

Monitoring & Enforcement

+

NGOs often act as watchdogs, monitoring whether governments and corporations are complying with international laws and norms. Example: Human Rights Watch's reports on abuses worldwide.

Service Delivery

+

Many NGOs are on the front lines of delivering humanitarian aid and development assistance, often in areas where states are unwilling or unable to act. Example: Doctors Without Borders providing medical care in conflict zones.

The Counter-Movement

Globalization has not been a smooth or uncontested process. Since the late 1990s, a powerful and diverse "anti-globalization" or "anti-capitalist" movement has emerged to challenge the dominant neoliberal model.

Who Are They?

A loose, global network of activists, environmentalists, trade unionists, indigenous groups, and anarchists. A "movement of movements."

What Are Their Criticisms?

They argue that neoliberal globalization deepens poverty, increases inequality, destroys the environment, and promotes corporate power at the expense of democracy.

Key Events

The 1999 "Battle of Seattle" against the WTO is seen as the movement's coming-out party. The World Social Forum provides an alternative venue to the elite World Economic Forum.

The Nation in a Global Age

Has globalization created a single "global village," erasing national identities? Or has it provoked a backlash, strengthening the forces of nationalism? The evidence is contradictory.

The Rise of Cosmopolitanism

The idea that we are "citizens of the world." Globalization, migration, and cultural exchange may be fostering a post-national sense of identity, where people feel a connection to all of humanity.

The Persistence of Nationalism

For many, globalization is seen as a threat to their culture and way of life. This can lead to a powerful backlash in the form of resurgent nationalism, religious fundamentalism, and anti-immigrant sentiment.
