Chapter 11: Nuclear Proliferation & Disarmament

The Bomb and World Politics

The Dawn of the Nuclear Age

The atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki in 1945 ushered in the nuclear age. For the first time, humanity possessed the ability to destroy itself. Nuclear weapons are not just more powerful conventional weapons; they are a revolution in military affairs that has reshaped global politics.

The key feature of nuclear weapons is their immense, city-destroying destructive power.

The Logic of Nuclear Strategy

Because nuclear war is unwinnable, the central goal of nuclear strategy is not to fight a war, but to prevent one from ever happening. This is the logic of deterrence.

First-Strike Capability

The ability to launch a nuclear attack that is so overwhelming it destroys an adversary's ability to retaliate.

Second-Strike Capability

The ability to withstand a first strike and still be able to launch a devastating counter-attack. This is the key to stable deterrence.

Mutually Assured Destruction (MAD)

A condition of stability where both sides possess a secure second-strike capability. This ensures that any nuclear attack would be suicidal, thus making it irrational to ever launch one.

Nuclear Proliferation: More May Be Better?

Nuclear proliferation is the spread of nuclear weapons to new actors. For decades, the central debate has been whether this spread makes the world more or less dangerous.

The Case AGAINST Proliferation (The 'Orthodox' View)

This view argues that proliferation is highly dangerous. New nuclear states may be unstable or irrational, lack secure second-strike capabilities, and increase the chances of accidental war or nuclear terrorism.

The Case FOR Proliferation (The 'Revisionist' View)

This controversial view, most associated with Kenneth Waltz, argues that "more may be better." Nuclear weapons are the ultimate deterrent; by acquiring them, states can guarantee their survival and create a powerful incentive for peace.

Controlling the Bomb

The international community has made significant efforts to control the spread of nuclear weapons and reduce the danger of nuclear war, with mixed success.

Arms Control

Measures designed to limit the number and type of weapons, or to control how they are used. The cornerstone of the arms control regime is the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT) of 1968, which sought to prevent the spread of nuclear weapons beyond the five states that already had them.

Disarmament

The goal of reducing or completely abolishing weapons. While complete nuclear disarmament remains a distant dream, significant progress was made after the Cold War, with the US and Russia drastically reducing the size of their arsenals through treaties like START (Strategic Arms Reduction Treaty).
