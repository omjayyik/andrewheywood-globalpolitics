Chapter 12: Terrorism

A Global Challenge

What is Terrorism?

Terrorism is a deeply contested concept, summed up by the clich√©, "one person's terrorist is another person's freedom fighter." However, most definitions agree on several key features that distinguish it from other forms of political violence.

It is Clandestine

Perpetrated by non-state actors operating secretly.

It Targets Civilians

It deliberately blurs the line between combatants and non-combatants.

It Aims to Create Fear

The violence is a symbolic act designed to intimidate a wider audience.

It is Political

The ultimate goal is not just violence, but to achieve a political objective.

The Evolution of Terrorism

The nature of terrorism has changed significantly over time, leading many to distinguish between 'old' and 'new' forms of terrorism.

'Old' Terrorism (pre-1990s)

Primarily nationalist or ideological in character (e.g., IRA, PLO, Red Brigades). Its goals were often negotiable political demands, and violence was typically selective and targeted to achieve maximum propaganda effect without alienating potential supporters.

'New' Terrorism (post-1990s)

Characterized by the rise of religious fundamentalism, especially radical Islamism (e.g., al-Qaeda, ISIS). Its goals are often absolutist and non-negotiable, and it embraces mass, indiscriminate violence to cause catastrophic damage. It operates as a diffuse, global network.

Significance: A Global Threat?

How significant is the threat of terrorism? Scholars are deeply divided on its importance in global politics.

Argument: Terrorism is a Major Threat

+

This view argues that 'new' terrorism represents a profound and unprecedented challenge. It is global in reach, exceptionally destructive, and poses a catastrophic threat, especially if terrorists were to acquire weapons of mass destruction (WMD).

Argument: Terrorism is an Exaggerated Threat

+

This view argues that terrorism is a "weapon of the weak" and that the threat is often exaggerated by governments and the media for political purposes. Statistically, people are far more likely to die from common diseases or car accidents than from a terrorist attack.

Countering Terrorism

How should states respond to the threat of terrorism? Two broad strategies have dominated the debate.

The 'War on Terror' Approach

This strategy, adopted by the US after 9/11, treats terrorism as a military problem. It emphasizes strengthening state security, using military force against terrorist groups and their state sponsors, and prioritizing security over civil liberties (e.g., through increased surveillance).

The 'Root Causes' Approach

This strategy treats terrorism as a political and social problem. It emphasizes tackling the underlying grievances that lead to terrorism, such as poverty, political oppression, and foreign intervention. This approach advocates for "winning hearts and minds" through development aid, diplomacy, and promoting justice.
