Chapter 16: Global Environmental Issues

Politics of the Planet

The 'Tragedy of the Commons'

Many environmental problems arise from the 'tragedy of the commons'. This concept describes a situation where individual actors, acting rationally in their own self-interest, deplete a shared public resource, even when it is not in anyone's long-term interest to do so. The "commons" are areas and resources that belong to no single state, such as the high seas, the atmosphere, and Antarctica.

Reformist Ecology: Working Within the System

This is the mainstream approach. It believes that environmental problems can be solved without fundamentally changing our way of life. It seeks a balance between economic growth and environmental protection through the concept of sustainable development—"development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs."

Radical Ecology: A New Worldview

This perspective argues that the environmental crisis is not a technical problem, but a symptom of a sick society. It calls for a radical transformation of our values, lifestyles, and economic system. This includes "deep ecologists" who reject anthropocentrism (the idea that humans are the center of the universe) in favor of ecocentrism (a nature-centered worldview).

The Ultimate Collective Problem: Climate Change

Climate change, caused by the emission of greenhouse gases from human activity, is arguably the greatest challenge facing humanity. It is a classic 'tragedy of the commons' problem, as the costs of climate change are global, but the benefits of burning fossil fuels are national and individual.

Global Environmental Governance

The international community has struggled to create effective mechanisms to tackle global environmental problems. Progress has been slow and often blocked by conflicts of interest.

The North–South Divide

The biggest obstacle to international cooperation. Developing countries in the Global South argue that developed countries in the North are historically responsible for the crisis and should bear the greatest burden. They also argue that they have a "right to develop" and should not have to sacrifice their economic growth for a problem they did not create.

Key Agreements

Despite the challenges, some progress has been made through international treaties. The 1997 Kyoto Protocol was the first to set binding targets for greenhouse gas emissions, but it was weakened by the refusal of the USA to ratify it. The 2015 Paris Agreement took a different approach, with all countries making voluntary commitments to reduce their emissions.
