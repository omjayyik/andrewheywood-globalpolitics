Chapter 2: The Historical Context

Making of the Modern World

From Westphalia to WWI

The modern global system wasn't born overnight. It evolved over centuries, starting with the birth of the sovereign state and expanding through an era of European dominance. Click each event to explore.

1648: The Peace of Westphalia

+

This treaty, ending the Thirty Years' War in Europe, is seen as the birth of the modern state system. It established the principle of state sovereignty, meaning that states have the right to control their own affairs without external interference. This created the "Westphalian system" of independent, self-governing states.

18th-19th Centuries: The Age of Imperialism

+

European powers, fueled by industrialization and nationalism, expanded their influence across the globe, creating vast colonial empires. This "first world/second world" divide established patterns of economic and political dominance that still resonate today.

1914-1918: The 'Great War' (WWI)

+

The collapse of the European balance of power, fueled by intense nationalism and imperial rivalries, led to a devastating war. Its aftermath saw the collapse of empires, the rise of the USA as a world power, and the first attempt at global governance: the League of Nations.

The Cold War Era (1945-90)

After WWII, a new global structure emerged: a bipolar world dominated by two superpowers, the USA and the USSR. This ideological struggle between capitalism and communism defined international politics for nearly half a century.

Containment

The core US strategy to prevent the spread of communism by "containing" it within its existing borders.

Proxy Wars

The superpowers avoided direct conflict but fought each other indirectly by backing opposing sides in regional conflicts (e.g., Korea, Vietnam, Afghanistan).

DÃ©tente

A period of eased tensions in the late 1960s and 1970s, characterized by arms control agreements and increased cooperation.

The 'Second' Cold War

A renewal of superpower hostility in the late 1970s and early 1980s, which ultimately led to the collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991.

The Post-Cold War Era

The collapse of the Soviet Union ushered in a new, uncertain era. Initial optimism about a "new world order" soon gave way to new challenges and a reshaping of global power.

A Unipolar Moment

The USA emerged as the world's sole superpower, leading to a period of unrivaled American dominance or "hegemony" in the 1990s.

The Rise of Others

The 21st century has seen the rise of new global players, especially China, leading to debates about a shift towards a multipolar world with several great powers.

New Security Challenges

The post-Cold War world saw a rise in intra-state conflicts (civil wars), humanitarian crises, and the emergence of "rogue states" and non-state actors as major security threats.

9/11 and the 'War on Terror'

The September 11, 2001 attacks marked a pivotal turning point, ushering in a new era of global politics defined by the threat of terrorism and the controversial "war on terror."

A New Kind of Threat

The attacks highlighted the danger posed by non-state actors like al-Qaeda, which operate globally and defy traditional state-based security responses.

The Bush Doctrine

In response, the US adopted a new, more aggressive foreign policy, characterized by a willingness to act pre-emptively and unilaterally, as seen in the 2003 invasion of Iraq.
