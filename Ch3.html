Chapter 3: Theories of Global Politics

Explaining a Complex World

Why Do We Need Theories?

The world is a messy, confusing place. Theories are like maps or lenses that help us make sense of it. They provide a systematic framework for understanding why events happen and how the world works. A theory is a proposition or set of propositions that seeks to explain phenomena.

The Mainstream Theories

Two theories have dominated the study of international politics for decades: Realism and Liberalism. They offer fundamentally different views of the world.

Realism: A World of Power and Conflict

Realism sees global politics as a relentless struggle for power among self-interested states. It's a pessimistic view, emphasizing conflict and competition.

  • Key Actors: States are the only actors that matter.
  • Human Nature: Humans are selfish and power-seeking.
  • Core Goal: Survival. States will do whatever is necessary to protect their national interest.
  • Key Concept: Anarchy. There is no higher authority than the state, leading to a self-help system.
  • Main Idea: The balance of power is the only guarantee of stability. Conflict is inevitable.

Liberalism: A World of Cooperation and Progress

Liberalism is more optimistic. It argues that cooperation is possible and that international institutions, economic interdependence, and democracy can create a more peaceful world.

  • Key Actors: States are important, but so are international organizations and non-state actors.
  • Human Nature: Humans are rational and capable of cooperation.
  • Core Goal: Progress and prosperity for all.
  • Key Concept: Interdependence. States are increasingly connected, making war costly and cooperation beneficial.
  • Main Idea: A "cobweb" of interdependence makes peace and cooperation the rational choice.

The Critical Theories

In recent decades, new theories have emerged to challenge the mainstream. These "critical" theories seek to uncover hidden power structures and offer alternative perspectives on global politics.

Marxism

Sees global politics as a struggle not between states, but between social classes. The global capitalist system is inherently exploitative, creating a world of haves (the core) and have-nots (the periphery).

Constructivism

Argues that the world is socially constructed. Our ideas, beliefs, and norms shape our reality. "Anarchy is what states make of it." Interests are not fixed; they are shaped by identity and culture.

Feminism

Highlights the role of gender in global politics. It argues that mainstream theories have ignored the perspectives and experiences of women, and that gender inequality shapes issues from war to development.
